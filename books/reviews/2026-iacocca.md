I read this book quite some time ago but never got around to reviewing it, so my memory might have faded quite a bit.

Here's what I remember:
- I like how the book is written in a way that feels like the author is speaking to you. You can feel the words coming off the page—it almost feels like reading the transcript of a speech.
- The chapter about competing with Japanese cars. I got a sense of how he treats Japan as a constructive competitor. He didn't resent Japan for making better and cheaper cars that Americans preferred to buy; rather, he lamented that American car manufacturers didn't achieve the same.
- I got to experience the putting-out-fires type of work that the head of a major company has to do—the part where he lobbied Congress for financial support amidst Chrysler's crisis was quite memorable.

I haven't read a lot of autobiographies by CEOs. The other recent one is [Bob Iger's](https://hci.prof/books/#2023-ride-of-a-lifetime). I guess most CEO books, like Iger's, fall on the spectrum of storytelling ("remember that time I did X"), but Iacocca's feels like more than that—it feels more intense, more purposeful, more "in your face." Although I am far from his line of work and probably can't learn much practically from his experience, I got to experience his career vividly through reading this book. Maybe that's why it stands out.
