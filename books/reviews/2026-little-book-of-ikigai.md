I wanted to read some books on ikigai—the famous Japanese philosophical framework said to guide you toward a purposeful life, a fulfilling career, and physical and mental health, among many other things. I chose this particular book mainly because it was written by a Japanese author in English. Most other ikigai books were written by Westerners in English, and I found it hard to convince myself to trust a non-Japanese interpretation of something so deeply integral to their culture.

Here, then, is *The Little Book of Ikigai*. It is indeed very short—probably finishable in a single sitting.

Overall, I appreciate how the book is filled with a wide variety of Japanese stories, from Jiro's sushi to sumo wrestling. The author tends to first tell such a story and then, at certain points, pause to note that here is what ikigai is about. I almost want to call the approach "evidence-based" or "grounded" (I can't help it—I am an academic who likes to use such intellectual terms). Rather than feeding readers a theoretical framework upfront, the author draws on concrete examples to materialize the theory.

Of course, the downside of this approach is the loss of a definitive framing. There are a few bullet points outlining what ikigai is about, but it almost seems that the author intentionally avoids presenting ikigai as such a list. My assumption is that ikigai, like many culturally unique philosophies, is hard to pin down in succinct language—let alone in a foreign one. And so the author might prefer that readers take away the rich stories that embody ikigai, rather than an oversimplified, slogan-like version of it.

Some quotes I highlighted:

> In my view, *ikigai* is a kind of cognitive and behavioural hub, around which various life habits and value systems are organized.

On *kodawari*—a pursuit of perfection:

> *Kodawari* is a personal standard, to which the individual adheres in a steadfast manner. It is often, though not always, used in reference to a level of quality, or professionalism to which the individual holds. It is an attitude, often maintained throughout one's life, constituting a central element of *ikigai*.

> What is perhaps unique in Japan is the prevalence of the *kodawari* spirit among ordinary people. From small owners of *izakaya* taverns to producers of Kobe beef and tuna from Ōma (a port in the Aomori prefecture, in the north of the country), Japan has a large population of people expressing their own *kodawari*.

On *ichigo ichie*:

> ...*ichigo ichie* (literally meaning 'one time, one encounter') originally comes from the tea ceremony tradition. Rikyū is the most likely originator of this important idea. *Ichigo ichie* is the appreciation of the ephemeral character of any encounters with people, things or events in life. Precisely because an encounter is ephemeral, it must be taken seriously. Life, after all, is filled with things that happen only once. The realization of the 'onceness' of life's encounters and pleasures provides the foundation of the Japanese conceptualization of *ikigai*, and is central to the Japanese philosophy of life.
