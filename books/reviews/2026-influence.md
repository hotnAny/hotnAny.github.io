This book is another testimony that behavioral science wins.

The study of how human behavior is influenced has direct applications and implications in so many scenarios, from selling a product to negotiating a raise.

I appreciate how each chapter is discussed in great depth, although sometimes it feels like it drags on too long and loses some saliency, diluting the main takeaway.

Perhaps the most familiar and memorable "trick" is that simply labeling the most popular items will automatically increase people's tendency to choose them, such as highlighting the top three dishes on a menu. The magic of this behavior is that 1) you can simply tell the truth—no lying, no hand-waving; and 2) only the relative popularity matters—it works even if your entire menu is unpopular; highlighting the best items among them can still draw more traffic.

Another familiar trick is scarcity: showing how likely an item is to become unavailable can automatically nudge people over the fence of hesitation.

Make people make commitments: if they commit to a cause without any specific action earlier, they are more likely to commit to and follow through on a specific action for that cause. The dark pattern of this is that car salesmen sometimes offer a deal to lure your commitment. After going through a ton of paperwork, you are informed that the deal isn't available due to an error and you can choose to back out. But most people are likely to come through anyway, as they have already made a series of commitments.

The book mentions Kahneman's System I vs. System II thinking, which I think is the fundamental, cross-cutting theory that explains why people can be manipulated and influenced by these tricks. If only we were programmed to always think logically (System II).

How to use this book:
- Review the key principles next time you go buy a car or a TV—something expensive enough for some salespeople to use those tricks on you.
- Maybe: as a manager, you can use those techniques to convince/persuade your team to follow you—for example, invoking social proof by highlighting practices that other successful teams have adopted, asking for small commitments (agreeing on a goal in principle) before making bigger asks, or framing the scarcity of an opportunity (a limited window to ship something) to nudge people over their hesitation.
