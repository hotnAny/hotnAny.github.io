This book consists of a collection of loosely connected essays in which the author develops theories about how the human mind works.

I gave up on this book.

I’ve tried to read it twice. The first time, I read one essay each morning as part of my routine. It didn’t stick for long, although I made it to about a quarter of the book. The second time, I started over. Since each essay is only one page, it seemed reasonable to use the same strategy. This time, I read one essay per day as part of my evening routine. I almost made it to the end—perhaps with about one-fifth left.

But I quit anyway.

Not because it was too grueling to read one page a day, or too repetitive to maintain the routine, but because I reached a tipping point: I no longer believed that the kind of theory this book attempts to develop can be effectively conveyed—or learned—through a collection of loosely connected essays.

Don’t get me wrong. I’m a big fan of modular, small-bite formats—short chapters that can be consumed in one sitting, especially as our attention spans continue to shrink. But I don’t think that format works well here. The book is developing a theory of how the human mind works, without experimental validation. Both the topic (the human mind) and the nature of the work (purely theoretical) demand deeper, more structured engagement with the reader.

What’s needed, in my view, is something closer to a textbook—built on an underlying knowledge structure, where readers can see how ideas accumulate and connect over time. Unfortunately, that structure is missing. Although the essays are grouped into chapters, there is little intrinsic organization within or across them. There’s no real sense of progression or buildup from one essay to the next. Worse, the author frequently refers back to concepts introduced earlier, seemingly expecting the reader to construct their own mental map to connect everything.

Overall, I still think Minsky is brilliant. Compared to Jeff Hawkins, who also developed his own theory of the brain, Minsky’s work feels more grounded in detailed, illustrative scenarios and toy examples. I just wish this book had been presented in a more tightly structured form.
