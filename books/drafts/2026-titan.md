this is a formiddably thick book (over 200,000 words). for the first time, i asked LLM to help me decide which chapters to read and the recommendation was chapter 6, 11, and 13. so that what i read

the writing is polished. the attention to details is impressive. what's more impressive is how such a standard of polish and detail-orientedness was maintained throughout the entire book. I can't imagine the amount of research done to put together so many detailed, vivid descriptions and the shear efforts of writing every one of them well.

the Rockefeller's parenting style is quite modern---
> Just as Standard Oil workers never remembered a cross word from John Senior, so Junior couldn't cite a single instance of paternal anger. His father was patient and encouraging, if notably stingy with praise. As junior said, his father was a "beloved companion. He had a genius with children. He never told us what to do or not to do. He was one with us." ... John and Cettie never administered coporal punishment, and they inculcated moral principles by instruction and example. Each child was taught to listen to his or her conscience as a severely infallible guide.

this quote pretty much sums up Rockfeller's "screte sauce"---
> "It is chiefly to my confidence in men and my ability to inspire their confience in me that I owe my success in life."

Rockefeller's contribution to the world
> That he created one of the first multinational corporations, selling kerosene around the world and setting a business pattern for the next century, was arguably his greatest feat.

overall, although i am not well pre-conditioned to resonate well with Rockefeller's time or his career, i nonetheless get the sense that he represents the type of "American dream"---how a person, without an advantaged starting point, can work hard and resourcefully to gradually become more and more successful, seemingly without a ceiling.
