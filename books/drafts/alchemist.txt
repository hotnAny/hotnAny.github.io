Believe it or not, this book sat under the passenger seat of my car. I read it only during fragments of time while I had to sit in the car and didn't have other things that I had to do, such as a 10-minute wait at the parking lot before picking up my kids. So I managed to finish the rather short book in about 2.5 years' time.

Some impressions:

- The writing (or rather, the way the characters speak as the book has a lot of dialogs) is so calm and peaceful even after translation from the original language to English. such calmness and peace instill wisdom, mainly because it feels words have been well thought out before spoken.

- The part that resonated with me the most is the boy's intersection with the glassware merchant, although this might not have been meant to be the climax--it's in the middle of the book. when reading this part, i can feel how the merchant's life and thought mirror that of the boy's, reinforcing the tension between one's personal legend and the inertia of being dragged by the reality in one's life.

- I only marked one place of quote
> It said that all people who are happy have God within them. And that happiness could be found in a grain of sand from the dessert, as the alchemist had said. Because a grain of sand is a moment of creation, and the universe has taken millions of years to create it. "Everyone on earth has a treasure that awaits him," his heart said, "We, people's hearts seldom say much about those treasures, because people no longer want to go in search of them. We speak of them only to children. Later, we simply let life proceed, in its own direction, toward its own fate. But, unfortunately, very few follow the path laid out for them--the path to their Personal Legends, and to happiness. Most people see the world as a threatening place, and because they do, the world turns out, indeed, to be a threatening place.

- the ending is unsurprisingly surprising and the natural take-away seems to be it's the journey that matters. the Personal Legend of the boy is not necessarily succeeding in getting the treasure--he went through the series of adventure only to find out that the treasure was buried somewhere where he originally comes from. so theoretically he could've gotten the treasure without all the trouble but that wouldn't feel like realizing one's personal legend, would it?

I hope there are more Aha! moments in the book. Maybe I missed them. Maybe I wasn't ready to get them. Likely I need to re-read this book.
