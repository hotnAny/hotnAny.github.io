i read this book quite some time ago but never got around to review it so my memory might have been lost quite a bit.

here's what i remember:
- i like how the book is written in a way that feels like the author is speaking to you. you can feel the words coming out of the page. almost feels like reading a transcript of a speech.
- the chapter about competing with japanese cars. i got a sense of how he treats japan as a constructive competitor. he didn't resent japan for making better and cheaper cars that americans preferred to buy but he more lamented how american car manufacturers didn't achieve that as well
- i got to experience the putting-out-fire type of work that the head of a major company would have to do--the part where he tried to lobby financial support from the congress amidst chryssler's crisis was quite memorable.

i haven't read a lot of autobiographies from CEOs. the other recent one is Bob Iger's (http://127.0.0.1:8888/books/#2023-ride-of-a-lifetime).
i guess most CEO books, like Iger's, fall on the spectrum of storytelling ("remember that time i did X") but Iacocca's feels more than that---it feels more intense, more purposeful, more "in your face". although i am far from his line of work and probably can't really learn much practically from his experience, i got to experience his career vividly via reading this book. maybe that's why it stands out.
