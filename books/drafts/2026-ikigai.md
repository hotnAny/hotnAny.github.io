i wanted to read some books on ikigai--the famous japanese philosophical framework that'd guide you to live a purposeful life, pursue a fulfilling career, stay physically and mentally healthy, amongst many other things

i chose this book mainly because it was written by a japanese author in english. most other ikigai books were written by westerners in english and i found it hard to convince myself to trust a non-japanese's interpretation of something so deeply integral to their culture.

here goes the little book of ikigai. it is indeed very short, probably can be finished in one sitting.

overall, i appreciate how the book is filled with a large variety of japanese stories---from Jiro's sushi to Sumo wrestling. the author would first tell such stories and then, at certain points, point out that here is what ikigai is about. i almost want to call it "evidence-based" or "grounded" (I can't help--i am an academic who like to use such intellectual terms). rather than feeding users a theoretical framework upfront, i appreciate how the author draw from concrete examples to materialize theory.

of course, the downside of this approach is the diminishing of a definitive framing. there are a few bullets listed outlining what ikigai is about but it almost seems that the author intentionally try not to present ikigai as such a bulleted list. my assumption is that ikigai, like many other culturally-unique philosophy, is hard to pin down using succinct language, not to mention a foreign language. thus the author might would like the readers to take away the rich stories that embody ikigai, rather than an over-simplified slogan-like version of it.

some notes i took:

> In my view, *ikigai* is a kind of cognitive and behavioural hub, around which various life habits and value systems are organized.

About kodawari--a pursuit of perfection
> *Kodawari* is a personal standard, to which the individual adheres in a steadfast manner. It is often, though not always, used in reference to a level of quality, or professionalism to which the individual holds. It is an attitude, often maintained throughout one's life, constituting a central element of *ikigai*

> What is perhaps unique in Japan is the prevalence of the *kodawari* spirit among ordinary people. From small owners of *izakaya* taverns to producers of Kobe beef and tuna from Ōma (a port in the Aomori prefecture, in the north of the country), Japan has a large population of people expressing their own *kodawari*.

About ichigo ichie---
> ... *ichigo ichie* (literally meaning 'one time, one encounter') originally comes from the tea ceremony tradition. Rikyū is the most likely originator of this important idea. *Ichigo ichie* is the appreciation of the ephemeral character of any encounters with people, things or events in life. Precisely because an encounter is ephemeral, it must be taken seriously. Life, after all, is filled with things that happen only once. The realization of the 'onceness' of life's encounters and pleasures provides the foundation of the Japanese conceptualization of *ikigai*, and is central to the japanese philosophy of life.
