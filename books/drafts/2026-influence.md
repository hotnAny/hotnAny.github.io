this book is another testimony that behavioral science wins.

the study of human behaviors of being influenced has direct application and implication in so many scenarios from selling a product to negotiating a raise.

i appreciate how each chapter was discussed in greath depth, although sometimes it feels dragging too long and losing some saliency, diluting the main take-away

perhaps the most familiar and memorable "trick" is simply labeling the most popular items will automatically increase people's tendency to choose them, such as highlighting the top 3 dishes on the menu. the magic of this behavior is 1) you can simply tell the truth, no lying no hand-waing; 2) only the relative popularity matters--it works even if your entire menu items are unpopular--highlighting the best ones among them can sitll draw more traffic

another familiar trick is scarcity--showing how likely an item will be unavailable can automatically nudge people over the fence of hesitation.

make people make commitment: if they commit to a cause without any specific action earlier, they are more likely to commit to and following through a specific action for that course. the dark pattern of this is car salesmen sometimes offer a deal to lure your commitment. after going through a ton of paper work, you are informed that the deal isn't available due to an error and you can choose to back out. but most people are likely to come through anyway as they have made a series of commitment.

the book mentioned kahneman's system i vs. ii thinking, which i think is the fundamental and cross-cutting theories that explain why people can be manipulated and influenced by these tricks. if only we are programmed to always think logically (system ii).

how to use this book:
- review the key principles next time you go buy a car or a tv---something expensive enough for some sales persons to use those tricks on you
- maybe: as a manager, you can use those techniques to convince / persuade your team to follow you?
